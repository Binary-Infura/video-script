MODULE 1: FUNDAMENTALS OF AI VIDEO GENERATION 
 
Module 1: Fundamentals of AI Video Generation 
1.1 What is AI Video Generation? 
AI Video Generation is the process of using artificial intelligence models to create video content — 
either from scratch or by transforming existing media — using text descriptions, images, or other videos 
as input. Instead of manually animating or filming scenes, you describe what you want, and the AI 
generates frames, motion, and visual continuity automatically. 
 
Think of it like this: imagine you could whisper a movie scene description into a machine's ear and it 
renders a cinematic clip in seconds. That is essentially what AI video generation does. 
 
� PRO TIP 
AI video generation does NOT work like a video editor. It generates pixels by learning statistical 
patterns from billions of video frames during training. 
The quality of your OUTPUT is directly proportional to the quality of your INPUT prompt and the 
model you choose. 
 
1.2 How AI Models Work 
1.2.1 Diffusion Models 
Diffusion models are currently the most dominant approach in AI image and video generation. They 
work through a two-phase process: 
 
• 
Forward Diffusion (Training): The model is trained by gradually adding Gaussian noise to real 
video frames until they become pure noise. The model learns to recognize patterns at each 
noise level. 
• 
Reverse Diffusion (Inference/Generation): Starting from pure noise, the model iteratively 
removes noise step-by-step, guided by your text prompt, until a clean video frame emerges. 
 
Analogy: Imagine a sculptor who studies thousands of sculptures being crushed into dust. After 
learning the reverse process, they can take a pile of dust and reconstruct a sculpture from a description 
alone — that's diffusion in essence.

Diffusion models are highly controllable via prompts but computationally expensive. 
Number of denoising steps (inference steps) trades off speed vs quality. 
 
1.2.2 Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) 
GANs were the kings of video generation before diffusion models arrived. A GAN consists of two neural 
networks in competition: 
 
• 
Generator Network: Creates fake video frames from random noise. 
• 
Discriminator Network: Tries to distinguish real frames from generated (fake) ones. 
• 
Training Loop: Both networks improve simultaneously — the generator gets better at fooling the 
discriminator, and the discriminator gets better at detecting fakes. 
 
Analogy: Think of an art forger (generator) and an art critic (discriminator). The forger keeps improving 
until even the expert can't tell real from fake. 
 
GANs are fast at inference time and great for face synthesis, style transfer, and deepfake applications. 
However, they suffer from 'mode collapse' (generating repetitive outputs) and are harder to control via 
text prompts. 
 
1.2.3 Transformer-Based Models 
Transformers (the same architecture behind ChatGPT) are increasingly used for video generation. 
They treat video as sequences of tokens (patches of pixels across time) and use self-attention 
mechanisms to model long-range dependencies. 
 
• 
VideoGPT, CogVideo, and OpenAI's Sora all leverage transformer architectures. 
• 
Transformers can model temporal (time-based) relationships much better than older RNN-
based models. 
• 
Sora specifically uses 'video patches' — treating video like a language model treats words, 
which gives it exceptional scene coherence.

2.2 Resolution 
Resolution refers to the number of pixels in each frame, expressed as Width × Height. More pixels = 
more detail = larger file size = more processing power needed. 
 
• 
720p (1280×720): HD — Good for social media, fast generation, lower cost. 
• 
1080p (1920×1080): Full HD — Standard for YouTube, professional web content. 
• 
4K (3840×2160): Ultra HD — Professional production, cinema-quality output. 
• 
8K (7680×4320): Emerging standard — Extreme detail, currently impractical for most AI 
generators.

2.3 Aspect Ratio 
Aspect ratio is the proportional relationship between width and height of your video frame. It determines 
the visual 'shape' of your content. 
 
• 
16:9 (Widescreen) — Standard for YouTube, TV, cinematic feel. Most common for AI video. 
• 
9:16 (Vertical) — Instagram Reels, TikTok, YouTube Shorts. Mobile-first content. 
• 
1:1 (Square) — Instagram posts, balanced social media content. 
• 
21:9 (Cinematic Ultrawide) — Epic, movie-theater feel. Used for premium content. 
• 
4:3 (Classic TV) — Nostalgic, vintage feel. Rarely used in modern content. 
 
⚠️ COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID 
❌ Generating in 16:9 and then cropping for 9:16 — You lose half the frame and the AI's composition 
wasn't designed for vertical viewing. Always specify the correct ratio upfront. 
 
2.4 Bitrate 
Bitrate measures the amount of data processed per second in a video file, typically expressed in 
Megabits per second (Mbps). It directly controls visual quality vs file size. 
 
• 
Low bitrate (1-4 Mbps): Compression artifacts, blocky motion, good for streaming preview. 
• 
Medium bitrate (8-15 Mbps): Good quality for YouTube and web delivery. 
• 
High bitrate (25-50 Mbps): Professional broadcast quality. 
• 
Ultra-high bitrate (100+ Mbps): Raw/uncompressed production masters. 
 
For AI-generated video: always export at the highest bitrate your platform allows, then re-encode for 
delivery. You cannot recover lost quality from low-bitrate exports.

2.5 Latent Space 
Latent space is one of the most important concepts in AI generation. It is the compressed mathematical 
representation of all the visual concepts a model has learned. Instead of working directly with pixels, AI 
models operate in this compressed 'idea space'. 
 
Analogy: Imagine a massive library where every concept (dog, sunset, explosion, film noir) is stored as 
coordinates on an infinite map. When you write a prompt, the AI navigates to the corresponding 
coordinates and decodes them back into pixels. 
 
• 
Navigating latent space with prompts is what 'prompt engineering' fundamentally is. 
• 
Interpolation between two latent points = smooth transformation between two concepts. 
• 
Latent space distance: similar concepts are 'close' (dog and wolf), opposite concepts are 'far' 
(day and night). 
 
� PRO TIP 
When you 'mix' styles in a prompt ('cyberpunk samurai in the style of Monet'), you're asking the AI to 
find a point between two regions of latent space. The more specific your prompt, the more precisely 
you navigate. 
 
2.6 Prompt Tokens 
Tokens are the fundamental unit of text processing in AI models. A token is roughly 3/4 of a word in 
English. Your text prompt is split into tokens before the AI processes it. 
 
• 
'cinematic sunset over ocean' = approximately 5 tokens 
• 
Most AI video models accept 75–300 tokens per prompt 
• 
Tokens near the beginning of your prompt have MORE influence than tokens at the end 
• 
Each token occupies 'attention' resources — too many tokens dilute the model's focus 
 
� PRO TIP 
Put your MOST important visual elements (subject, action, style) in the FIRST 20 tokens of your 
prompt. The model pays more attention to early tokens.

The seed is a number that initializes the random noise from which the AI begins generating. Using the 
same seed with the same prompt and settings produces the same (or very similar) output. This is 
critical for reproducibility. 
 
• 
Fixed seed + same prompt = consistent, reproducible outputs 
• 
Different seeds = completely different variations of the same prompt 
• 
Seed exploration: run with 5-10 random seeds, pick the best, then refine 
• 
Most platforms allow manual seed setting — always record seeds of generations you like 
 
� SEED WORKFLOW EXAMPLE 
Step 1: Generate 5 variations with random seeds (0, 42, 1337, 8888, 99999) 
Step 2: Identify seed 1337 produces the best composition 
Step 3: Lock seed to 1337, now refine your prompt while keeping the same base noise 
Result: Controlled iteration without losing your best starting point 
 
2.8 Sampling Methods 
Sampling methods are the algorithms that determine HOW the AI traverses from noise to a finished 
image/frame during the denoising process. Different samplers produce different results with the same 
prompt and seed. 
 
• 
DDIM (Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models): Fast, deterministic, great for low step counts (20-30 
steps). Most commonly used for video. 
• 
DDPM (Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models): Original method, slower but sometimes 
higher quality. 
• 
DPM++ 2M Karras: High quality, efficient — current favorite for image generation. 
• 
Euler Ancestral (Euler a): Creative, slightly random, good for artistic generation. 
• 
LMS (Linear Multistep): Stable, good for complex scenes. 
 
For AI video: DDIM is the most widely used sampler because it's deterministic (consistent between 
frames) which helps temporal consistency. Stochastic samplers can cause flickering between frames. 
 
2.9 Noise & Denoising 
Noise in AI video generation refers to random pixel information (like TV static) that serves as the 
starting point for generation. Denoising is the process of iteratively removing this noise while guided by 
your prompt to produce a coherent image.

2.10 Temporal Consistency 
Temporal consistency refers to how smoothly and coherently elements maintain their appearance, 
position, and properties across consecutive video frames. It is THE primary challenge in AI video 
generation. 
 
Without temporal consistency: characters change face shape between frames, objects flicker, 
backgrounds shift colors, and motion looks jittery — this is the hallmark of low-quality AI video. 
 
• 
Techniques for better temporal consistency: optical flow guidance, attention sharing across 
frames, latent interpolation, fixed random seeds per scene 
• 
Models with best temporal consistency in 2024-2025: Sora, Runway Gen-3, Kling 1.5 
• 
Prompt technique: explicitly mention 'consistent character', 'stable environment', 'smooth motion' 
 
2.11 Motion Coherence 
Motion coherence is the physical plausibility and smoothness of movement in generated video. It 
encompasses whether objects move in physically believable ways — acceleration, gravity, fluid 
dynamics, rigid body behavior. 
 
• 
High motion coherence: A ball bounces with realistic arc, water flows naturally, hair moves with 
wind. 
• 
Low motion coherence: Objects teleport between frames, physics-defying movements, limbs 
bend impossibly. 
• 
AI models trained on more video data tend to have better learned physics — Sora was trained 
on massive video datasets specifically for this.

❌ Prompting complex physics actions (water splashes, fire, explosions) with small/fast models —

2.12 Keyframes 
Keyframes are specific frames in a video that define the 'anchor' state of a scene — the starting point, 
an important mid-point, or the end state. The AI or interpolation algorithm fills in the frames between 
keyframes. 
 
• 
Keyframe 0: Your character stands at the door 
• 
Keyframe 24: Your character opens the door 
• 
Keyframe 48: Your character walks through 
• 
Between keyframes, the AI interpolates smooth motion 
 
In modern AI video tools, you can often specify image keyframes — providing the AI with your first and 
last frames, and letting it generate the transition between them. This gives you enormous control over 
the final output. 
 
� PRO TIP 
Use image-to-image to generate your keyframes first, then use them as anchors in video generation. 
This dramatically improves consistency and gives you precise control over scene composition. 
 
2.13 Interpolation 
Interpolation is the mathematical process of generating 'in-between' frames to create smooth transitions 
between keyframes or to increase frame rate. Two main types: 
 
• 
Linear Interpolation (LERP): Simple averaging between frames. Fast but can look mechanical. 
• 
Optical Flow Interpolation: Analyzes pixel motion between frames and generates intermediate 
frames that follow the actual motion vectors. Much smoother and more realistic. 
• 
AI Frame Interpolation (FILM, RIFE, DAIN): Deep learning models trained specifically on frame 
interpolation. Can smoothly interpolate even very different frames.

2.14 Rendering 
Rendering is the computational process of converting AI model outputs (mathematical representations, 
latent codes) into actual pixel values that form your final video frames. In AI video generation, rendering 
happens in multiple stages. 
 
• 
Latent rendering: The AI generates the video in compressed latent space 
• 
Decoder rendering: A VAE (Variational Autoencoder) decodes latent representations into pixel-
level frames 
• 
Post-processing: Optional upscaling, color grading, noise reduction, sharpening 
• 
Encoding: Final frames are compressed into video format (H.264, H.265, ProRes, etc.) 
 
Generation time (often mistakenly called 'render time') depends on: resolution, frame count, number of 
denoising steps, model size, and hardware (GPU VRAM and speed). 
 
2.15 Style Transfer 
Style transfer is the technique of applying the visual aesthetic, artistic style, or 'look' of one source to 
another video or image. It separates CONTENT (what is depicted) from STYLE (how it looks). 
 
• 
Neural Style Transfer: Uses a content image and a style image, combining them mathematically 
• 
Prompt-based style transfer: Describe the style in your prompt ('in the style of Studio Ghibli', 
'cyberpunk neon aesthetic') 
• 
Video-to-video style transfer: Use an existing video as content, apply new style via model 
 
� STYLE TRANSFER EXAMPLES 
Content: A man walking in a city + Style: 'watercolor painting' → Watercolor animation 
Content: Product shot + Style: 'neon cyberpunk, high contrast' → Stylized ad 
Content: Interview footage + Style: 'vintage film grain, 1970s color grade' → Retro documentary feel

3.1 Diffusion Models — Deep Dive 
Diffusion models dominate modern AI video generation. Understanding their architecture helps you 
work with them more effectively. 
 
• 
U-Net Architecture: The backbone of most diffusion models. Encodes images to a compressed 
representation, performs denoising, then decodes back. The 'U' shape means information flows 
down (encoding) then back up (decoding) with skip connections. 
• 
CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training): The component that connects text prompts to 
visual concepts. CLIP was trained on 400 million image-text pairs and understands the 
relationship between words and visual elements. 
• 
VAE (Variational Autoencoder): Compresses full-resolution frames into a much smaller 'latent' 
representation (typically 8x smaller in each dimension). This makes generation feasible — 
instead of working with 1920×1080 pixels, the model works with 240×135 latents. 
• 
Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG): Controls how strongly the model follows your text prompt. 
CFG=1 = barely follows prompt. CFG=7 = balanced. CFG=15 = very strongly follows prompt but 
may over-saturate. 
 
� PRO TIP 
CFG Scale sweet spot for video: 7-9. Below 5 = too creative/random, above 12 = artifacts and over-
saturation. Experiment within this range. 
 
3.2 GANs — Generative Adversarial Networks 
While less dominant for text-to-video, GANs remain important for specific applications: 
 
• 
StyleGAN / StyleGAN2: State-of-the-art for face generation. Used in digital human creation. 
• 
VideoGAN: Extends GAN to temporal domain — still used for face animation. 
• 
CycleGAN: Unpaired image-to-image translation. Converts horse videos to zebra videos without 
needing matching pairs.

3.3 Transformer-Based Video Models 
The transformer architecture, originally designed for text (NLP), has been adapted for video generation 
with remarkable results. 
 
• 
Video Transformer: Treats each video frame as a sequence of image patches. Temporal 
attention allows the model to 'look across time' — frame 1 can attend to frame 30 to maintain 
consistency. 
• 
Spatiotemporal Attention: Combines spatial attention (within a frame) with temporal attention 
(across frames). This is key to Sora's exceptional temporal consistency. 
• 
Masked Video Modeling (like VideoMAE): Trains by masking random patches in video and 
predicting the missing content — similar to how BERT works for text. 
 
3.4 Multimodal Models 
Multimodal models accept multiple input types simultaneously — text, images, audio, video — and can 
generate across modalities. These represent the frontier of AI video generation. 
 
• 
Text + Image → Video: Use a reference image for character/style consistency while guiding 
motion with text 
• 
Video + Text → Enhanced Video: Input video with quality improvement instructions 
• 
Text + Audio → Synchronized Video: Generate video that rhythmically matches audio input

MODULE 4: PROMPT ENGINEERING FOR AI VIDEO 
 
Module 4: Prompt Engineering for AI Video 
Prompt engineering is the art and science of communicating with AI models to get precisely the output 
you envision. For video generation, this is far more complex than image prompting because you're 
directing motion, cinematography, AND visual content simultaneously. 
 
4.1 The Anatomy of a Perfect Video Prompt 
A professional AI video prompt has 9 distinct layers, each contributing to a different aspect of the final 
output: 
 
• 
1. SCENE: The overall setting and context. Where is this happening? What is the broad 
situation? 
• 
2. SUBJECT: Who or what is the main focus? Physical description, clothing, distinctive features. 
• 
3. ACTION: What is happening? What is the subject doing? What is the motion? 
• 
4. CAMERA: Camera angle, movement, lens choice, shot type. 
• 
5. LIGHTING: Quality, direction, color temperature, and mood of light sources. 
• 
6. ENVIRONMENT: Supporting environmental details, background elements, atmosphere. 
• 
7. STYLE: Artistic style, reference films/photographers/artists, visual aesthetic. 
• 
8. MOOD: The emotional feeling, tone, and atmosphere you want the viewer to feel. 
• 
9. QUALITY: Technical quality indicators — resolution, grain, format, professional level. 
 
� PRO TIP 
Write prompts in this order: Subject + Action + Camera + Lighting + Style. The AI processes tokens 
sequentially, so front-loading the most critical information ensures it gets the most attention weight.

7.1 Types of AI Avatars 
AI avatars are synthetic human representations that can speak, move, and interact. They range from 
simple animated images to photorealistic digital clones. 
 
• 
Stock Avatars: Pre-made AI characters available in platforms like HeyGen, Synthesia, D-ID. 
Easy to use, diverse options, consistent quality. Limitations: Not unique to your brand. 
• 
Custom AI Avatar Clones: Create a digital version of yourself or a specific person by recording 
2-5 minutes of video and training a custom model. Looks and sounds exactly like the original. 
• 
Illustrated/Animated Avatars: Cartoon or stylized AI characters. Used in edtech, gaming, and 
children's content. 
• 
Interactive Real-Time Avatars: AI avatars that respond in real-time to user input — used for 
customer service, virtual assistants, and interactive presentations. 
 
7.2 Creating Your AI Avatar — Step by Step 
1. Record 2-5 minutes of yourself speaking in a well-lit, neutral background environment 
2. Use a professional microphone (or quiet environment with a clean recording) 
3. Speak at varied paces, with varied facial expressions, looking directly at camera 
4. Upload to HeyGen, Synthesia, or D-ID 
5. Train your custom avatar model (usually 24-48 hours processing) 
6. Test with a short script — check for unnatural mouth movements, eye blinking, head turns 
7. Refine with longer, more varied training data if quality is insufficient

AI video tools are powerful but they don't automatically create compelling stories. Great AI video still 
requires human creative vision, structure, and emotional intelligence. 
 
• 
The Three-Act Structure applies to AI video just as much as Hollywood films: Setup (establish 
world/character), Confrontation (tension, challenge, development), Resolution (payoff, 
conclusion, emotional satisfaction) 
• 
Shot composition for storytelling: Use the rule of thirds, leading lines, and depth layers to create 
visually dynamic compositions even in AI-generated content 
• 
Pacing: Mix long takes (wide, contemplative shots) with rapid cuts (close-ups, action) for 
rhythmic storytelling 
• 
Color story: Use consistent color palettes across your AI generations to create visual coherence 
— warm tones for positive moments, cool tones for tension or sadness 
 
8.2 Scene Transitions 
Transitions between AI-generated scenes require thoughtful planning since you can't simply cut 
between wildly different visual styles without jarring the viewer. 
 
• 
Match Cut — End scene 1 with a shape/color/movement that mirrors the opening of scene 2 
(classic cinema technique) 
• 
Dissolve — Gradually fade between two overlapping scenes (softest transition) 
• 
Whip Pan — Generate both scenes with a whip pan motion at the cut point, creating speed-blur 
connection 
• 
Cut on Action — Cut at the moment of peak action for dynamic transitions 
• 
Color Bridge — Use a flash of color or light to connect two very different scenes 
• 
J-Cut / L-Cut — Audio from next scene starts before the cut (J-Cut) or continues after (L-Cut)

8.3 Camera Movement Techniques for AI 
Specifying camera movements in your AI video prompts is the single most powerful technique for 
elevating your output from amateur to cinematic. 
 
• 
The Reveal Technique: Start close on a detail, then pull back to reveal the full scene (generates 
viewer curiosity and satisfaction) 
• 
The Push-In: Camera slowly moves toward subject during emotional dialogue or important 
moments (creates intimacy and tension) 
• 
The 360° Orbit: Camera circles a subject, great for product shots and character introductions 
• 
The Dutch Angle Push-In: Tilted camera slowly approaching creates profound unease for 
horror/thriller 
• 
The Drone Reveal: Start low, rise dramatically to reveal landscape — epic for establishing shots 
 
8.4 Multi-Scene Consistency 
One of the hardest challenges in AI video production: keeping characters, environments, and styles 
consistent across multiple generated clips. 
 
• 
Character Reference Images: Generate a 'character sheet' — multiple views of your character 
in neutral pose — then use this as image-to-video input for every scene with that character 
• 
Style Keywords Lock: Create a 'style DNA' — a set of fixed style keywords that appear in 
EVERY scene prompt (e.g., 'photorealistic, warm cinematic grade, 35mm film grain, golden hour 
palette') 
• 
Environment Reference: Generate a wide shot of your key environment first, then use it as a 
reference image for all scenes set in that location 
• 
Seed Anchoring: Use the same or related seeds for scenes in the same environment to 
maintain visual consistency 
• 
Color Grade Lock: Post-process all generated clips through the same LUT (Look-Up Table) in 
your video editor to unify color grading

MODULE 11: LIMITATIONS & ETHICS 
 
Module 11: Limitations & Ethics of AI Video 
 
11.1 Current Technical Limitations 
• 
Temporal Inconsistency: Characters' faces, environments, and objects still 'drift' between frames 
in most models — a person's face may look slightly different 10 seconds later in the same clip. 
• 
Hand and Finger Generation: AI video still struggles with human hands — extra fingers, 
impossible joint configurations, and morphing between frames are common artifacts. 
• 
Text in Video: Generating readable text within video frames (signs, books, whiteboards) is still 
unreliable — text often appears as plausible-looking gibberish. 
• 
Long-Form Coherence: Beyond 30-60 seconds, narrative and character consistency break 
down significantly in most current models. 
• 
Complex Physics: Very specific physics interactions (liquid dynamics, shattered glass patterns, 
smoke behavior) are often physically inaccurate. 
• 
Generation Speed: High-quality video generation can take minutes to hours — real-time 
generation at high quality is still 2-3 years away. 
• 
Limited Control: You can describe what you want, but you can't precisely control every element 
— it's a probabilistic process. 
 
11.2 Deepfakes — Risks & Responsibility 
Deepfakes are perhaps the most discussed AI video concern. A deepfake is AI-generated video that 
depicts a real person saying or doing something they never said or did. 
 
• 
Political Deepfakes: Fabricated videos of politicians, world leaders, or candidates can influence 
elections and undermine democratic processes. 
• 
Fraud: Deepfakes of executives authorizing wire transfers have successfully defrauded 
companies of millions of dollars. 
• 
Non-Consensual Intimate Media: Creating intimate deepfakes of real people without consent is 
now illegal in many jurisdictions. 
• 
Reputation Destruction: False deepfakes of private individuals can cause devastating personal 
and professional harm.

11.3 Copyright Issues 
AI video generation raises significant unresolved copyright questions: 
 
• 
Training Data Copyright: AI models trained on copyrighted videos may be subject to legal 
challenges. Several lawsuits are pending against major AI companies. 
• 
Style Copyright: Generating video 'in the style of' a specific filmmaker may or may not infringe 
copyright — style itself is generally not copyrightable, but specific aesthetic elements might be. 
• 
Music Copyright: Using AI-generated music that sounds like a specific artist creates gray areas. 
Always use music from platforms that explicitly offer royalty-free licenses. 
• 
Output Copyright: In most jurisdictions (including the US as of 2025), purely AI-generated 
content without significant human creative input may not be copyrightable — meaning you may 
not own what you generate. 
• 
Safe Harbor: Use AI video tools that have licensed training data and provide clear commercial 
use rights in their terms of service. 
 
11.4 Bias in AI Video Models 
AI video models inherit and can amplify biases present in their training data: 
 
• 
Demographic Bias: Default character generation often skews toward certain ethnicities, body 
types, and ages. Explicitly specify diversity in your prompts. 
• 
Cultural Bias: Models trained predominantly on Western internet data may depict non-Western 
cultures through a Western lens with inaccuracies or stereotypes. 
• 
Gender Bias: Professional roles (CEO, doctor, scientist) may default to stereotyped gender 
presentations without explicit guidance.
